Chapter 7 · Part II — Controlling execution

Locks, queueing locks, priorities

Ten workers are a blessing until two of them sync the same customer's account at the same time. This chapter covers Procrastinate's three ordering tools: locks, which force jobs that share a resource to run one at a time; queueing locks, which stop duplicate jobs from piling up in the queue; and priorities, which decide who goes first when there's a line. It also includes a latency surprise we measured while writing it.

Locks: one at a time, in order

Set lock= to any string, at the task level or per-deferral with configure(). Jobs sharing a lock string execute serially: per the locks guide, "the second task cannot run before the first one has ended (successfully or not)," and among waiting locked jobs, higher priority goes first, then older ones. The lock string usually names the shared resource: a customer id, an account UUID, a file path.

The middle line is what you'd predict: two half-second jobs with nothing in common finish together. The first line is the surprise. Two half-second jobs sharing a lock took 5.6 seconds, not 1.1, because of how the handoff works: finishing a job doesn't notify workers that a lock has been released, so the second job waited for the worker's next scheduled poll of the database. At chapter 4's default fetch_job_polling_interval of 5 seconds, that's a worst-case 5-second gap between consecutive locked jobs. The third line is the same pair with 0.2-second polling: 1.2 seconds total. If your workload leans on locks for throughput-sensitive chains, the polling interval is now a latency knob, and its cost is one cheap query per tick.

⚠ Gotcha

Two blocking scenarios from the locks guide are worth memorizing. A locked job scheduled for the future blocks every later job on that lock until it runs. And a locked job stuck in doing because its worker died blocks the lock until something resolves it (chapter 12's stalled-job recovery). The one consolation is structural: "a job can only take a single lock so there's no dead-lock scenario possible," as the discussions page puts it.

Queueing locks: at most one in line

An execution lock doesn't stop the queue from filling with duplicates; it only slows their draining. For "don't pile these up" there's queueing_lock=: per the queueing-locks guide, at most one job with a given queueing lock can be in todo status. Deferring a second one raises AlreadyEnqueued:

Catch the exception and move on; a refused duplicate is the feature working. Two fine points: only todo counts, so a job that has started running (doing) no longer blocks new deferrals, and chapter 3's warning applies here: two jobs with the same queueing lock in one batch_defer roll the whole batch back.

Priorities: who goes first

Every job has an integer priority, default 0, settable on the task, in configure(), or from the CLI. Larger numbers win, per the priorities guide, and priority only orders jobs that are available: a worker won't idle waiting for a locked high-priority job when a lower-priority one is runnable, and nothing preempts a job that's already running.

🏭 In production

Priorities don't starve gracefully. The docs warn that in a system whose workers are always busy, a job with lower priority than the steady influx "could potentially wait months." If some class of work must always make progress, give it its own queue and its own workers (chapter 4) instead of a priority number. Capacity isolates; priority only reorders.
