Chapter 13 · Part IV — Production

Deploying workers on AWS ECS

The architecture is the one chapter 9 drew: web containers defer, worker containers execute, and the only shared infrastructure is Postgres — which on AWS means the RDS instance you likely already run. This chapter builds the worker image, proves its shutdown behavior locally with real signals, and walks through the ECS task definition line by line. The through-line is one deadline negotiation: ECS gives a stopping container a fixed grace period, and procrastinate has to finish or hand off its jobs inside it.

The image

The worker needs remarkably little: procrastinate, a driver, and your tasks module. Connection settings ride on the standard libpq environment variables from chapter 2, so the same image runs against local Postgres and RDS without modification.

Both comments in that file are scars, not decoration. Our first build used plain procrastinate and the container died on startup with exactly that ImportError, because python:3.12-slim ships no libpq and procrastinate's own dependency is the pure-Python psycopg. And the exec-form CMD is what makes the whole shutdown story below work at all: signals go to PID 1, and PID 1 must be the worker.

Prove the shutdown locally

docker stop sends SIGTERM, waits, then SIGKILLs (the same sequence ECS uses), so the deployment's most delicate behavior can be verified on a laptop. We started the container against the course database, deferred a five-second job, and stopped the container two seconds in:

That's chapter 12's graceful shutdown wearing its production clothes: SIGTERM arrives, the worker stops fetching, announces what it's waiting for, finishes the job, and exits on its own — well before the deadline. The job's status in the database afterward: succeeded.

The task definition

deploy/ecs-task-definition.json is the full Fargate task definition; these are the lines that carry weight. The numbers come from the ECS task definition parameters reference: stopTimeout is the "time duration (in seconds) to wait before the container is forcefully killed if it doesn't exit normally on its own" — 30 seconds when unset, 120 at most on Fargate.

The deadline negotiation lives in two numbers that must be read together. During a deploy or scale-in, ECS sends SIGTERM and waits stopTimeout seconds (120, the Fargate maximum) before SIGKILL. Inside that window, the worker waits up to its own --shutdown-graceful-timeout (90) for jobs to finish before it starts aborting them (chapter 8's machinery, with AbortReason.SHUTDOWN), and then needs a few seconds to record the aborts. The 30-second gap between 90 and 120 is that recording headroom. Get the inequality backwards and SIGKILL lands mid-abort: jobs freeze in doing, exactly the stalled state chapter 12 recovers from. That recovery recipe isn't optional garnish here; it's the backstop this whole design assumes.

The rest of the file, briefly. PGPASSWORD arrives through secrets (Secrets Manager), never plain environment. essential: true means a dead worker kills the task so the service replaces it. The health check reuses chapter 2's procrastinate healthchecks inside the container. And awslogs ships the worker's log (the same lines you've read all course) to CloudWatch. Run it all as an ECS service with a desired count, one service per queue group if chapter 4's routing matters to you; scaling is changing the count, since chapter 6 established that any worker can also carry the periodic scheduling.

🏭 In production

What this chapter verified is what a laptop can verify: the image builds, the worker runs against a real Postgres, and SIGTERM produces a clean handoff. The account-side steps (pushing to ECR, aws ecs register-task-definition, service creation, RDS networking and security groups) follow the same JSON you've just read, but only your AWS account can prove them. Treat the first deploy's kill-a-worker-mid-job test (chapter 12) as part of going live, not as optional.
