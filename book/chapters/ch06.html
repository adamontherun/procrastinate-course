Chapter 6 · Part II — Controlling execution

Scheduling: later and every hour

Two kinds of "later" show up in every system. One-off delays: send the follow-up email in three days, expire the trial in a month. And recurring schedules: rebuild the report every hour, clean up every night. Procrastinate handles the first with two arguments to configure(), and the second with a cron decorator whose most interesting property is what it doesn't need: a separate scheduler process.

One-off future jobs

A job scheduled for the future is an ordinary job row whose scheduled_at column is set. Per the scheduling guide, you set it either absolutely, with configure(schedule_at=some_datetime) — a naive datetime is rejected with ValueError: Timezone aware datetime is required (enforcement verified against 3.9.0) — or relatively, with configure(schedule_in={"hours": 1, "minutes": 30}), whose keys are Python timedelta arguments. Workers simply refuse to fetch a job before its time. The guide is precise about what the timestamp means: "If a job is configured with a date in the future, it will run at the first opportunity after that date." It's a floor, not an appointment; if every worker is busy at the scheduled moment, the job runs when one frees up.

The reminder sat in todo with its due time recorded, the periodic tick fired each second around it, and the reminder ran at its first opportunity past 23:54:31. Both features in one worker, no extra processes.

Periodic tasks

Stacking @app.periodic(cron="0 * * * *") on top of @app.task defers a job on that cron schedule: standard five-column syntax, with an optional sixth column for seconds as the demo above used. Every periodic task must accept a timestamp argument: the Unix timestamp of the moment the run was scheduled for, which is not necessarily the moment it runs. That's your anchor for idempotent work, like "build the report for the hour ending at timestamp."

The same task can carry several schedules: call app.periodic() as a function with different task_kwargs and a unique periodic_id per schedule, and one task definition becomes "sync region A every 5 minutes, region B every 10."

🔬 Under the hood

There is no scheduler process to deploy (Celery users: no beat). Each worker runs a "periodic deferrer" that computes upcoming cron ticks and defers the jobs itself. With five workers you have five deferrers racing, and the docs' guarantee is that "the database is responsible for making sure deferring only happens once per period, even with multiple workers." Concretely, the shipped schema puts a unique constraint on (task_name, periodic_id, defer_timestamp) in the procrastinate_periodic_defers table, turning the race into a no-op for the losers. Chapter 4's worker log showed the other side of this: "No periodic task found, periodic deferrer will not run."

⚠ Gotcha

Periodic scheduling happens inside workers, so if no worker is running, nothing gets deferred. On startup a worker catches up on missed schedules, but only recent ones: per the periodic-tasks guide, "a task that is more than 10 minutes late will not be deferred" (configurable through the App's periodic_defaults). If the whole worker fleet was down at midnight and came back at 00:30, the nightly job doesn't run that night; your deploy and incident processes should account for it.

One more interaction to plan for: a periodic task that's slower than its own period accumulates. A cleanup that takes two minutes, scheduled every minute, falls behind forever. The standard fix is chapter 7's queueing lock on the periodic task, so a new job is refused while the previous one still waits.
